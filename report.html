Five robot brains, one body

We took five of the most popular open-source AI models for controlling robots, put each one in charge of the same humanoid, and gave them the same job: pick up a block and set it down on a target. Identical training, 150 attempts each — 750 in total. Almost none of them could finish.

The four pre-trained models

Finishing outright was so rare that a pass/fail number tells you nothing. So we score how close the block actually got to the target on each attempt, where 100 would mean placed. The thin line through each bar is the margin of error: run the same model again and the score lands somewhere in that window. Where those lines overlap, the models are tied.

One clear winner, three genuinely equal. GR00T leads by about 12 points — comfortably outside the margins, so that gap is real. The other three sit within 1.0 point of each other with margins of ±3: a 4.1-billion-parameter model and a 450-million one, indistinguishable. That is not the measurement failing to tell them apart. With 150 attempts each we can resolve gaps of about 4 points, and there is no gap to find. Note also how far from solved this is — the leader closes barely a third of the distance.

Now add a model that never saw another robot

ACT is our control. It has 52 million parameters — 80× smaller than π0.5 — and no pre-training whatsoever. It never saw a robot dataset, a video corpus, or another machine. It learned this task from our 216 demonstrations and nothing else. Here is the same chart with it dropped in.

It lands second. The little model with no pre-training scores above all three of the other systems built on enormous robot datasets. Every advantage those models were supposed to bring — scale, data, months of GPU time — bought them nothing here.
How firm is that? ACT's lead over the three pre-trained models is 1.7 to 2.8 points against margins of about ±4 — too small to call on its own. The honest claim is not "the small model wins" but the harder-to-dismiss one: three of the four pre-trained models failed to beat a from-scratch baseline 80× smaller, and all three land on the same side of it.

Why would that happen?

1. ACT had no new body to learn. The pre-trained models learned on other robots — different arms, different cameras, different proportions. Before any of that knowledge helps here, it has to be translated onto this humanoid. ACT skipped the translation entirely: this robot is the only one it has ever known. For a single narrow task, that head start roughly cancels out having read the whole robot library.
2. The task is small enough to learn outright. 216 demonstrations of one motion is plenty for a 52M model to memorise. Pre-training pays off when you need to generalise — new objects, new instructions, new scenes. Nothing here asked for that, so the thing pre-training buys was never tested.
3. ACT plans in chunks and reacts faster. It predicts a burst of upcoming movements at once rather than one step at a time, which limits the drift that accumulates when a robot acts on its own last action. It also ran at 29.3 decisions per second against GR00T's 19.8 — a smaller model checks the world more often and corrects sooner.
4. But note what ACT did not do. It never once finished the task, and GR00T beat it clearly. So the honest reading isn't "small beats big" — it's that generic pre-training bought nothing here, while body-matched pre-training did. GR00T is the only model in this test that had already seen robots shaped like this one.

How sure are we?

Every score is an estimate from 150 attempts, so each comes with a range. Below, the dot is the measured score and the bar through it is where the true value almost certainly sits. If two ranges overlap, the models are tied — the difference between them is smaller than our ability to measure it.

The job has three stages. Only one is hard.

Those scores hide something important. Splitting them into the three stages shows the task doesn't get gradually harder — it hits a wall.

A cliff, not a slope. Picking up is solved — 100%, every model, every attempt. Carrying is a coin flip. Placing collapses to nothing. The entire difficulty lives in the last few centimetres: the target is 3.7 × 4.9 cm, about a matchbox.
And it fails sideways, not vertically. We assumed the killer was height — the target is only 5 mm tall. It isn't. At their closest approach the models are within 4 mm of the correct height and about 11 cm off to the side. They know how high to hold the block. They cannot get it over the right spot.

Three of them park. One drives past. ACT, SmolVLA, π0.5 and WALL-OSS all do the same thing: carry the block to roughly the right area, stop, and hold it there — half the episode — until the clock expires. They finish within a centimetre of the closest they ever got. They know where to go and cannot decide when to let go.
GR00T fails differently, and more interestingly. It makes the fastest and closest approach in the test — and then carries the block about 13 cm back out again, ending far from the best position it reached. It isn't loitering; it's overshooting. That is a stopping problem, not a seeing problem: the model that locates the target best is the one that can't halt on it. It is also the one case here where a crude fix — stop when close enough — might convert near-misses into successes.
Almost nobody fumbles. In 741 failed attempts the block ended up back down on the surface just twice, both by GR00T. Every other failure finished with the robot still holding it. Nothing was knocked away or abandoned — the grasp is not the problem.
Success is fast or never. The nine successes took 5.7 to 8.1 seconds — a fraction of the time allowed. Nobody ever found success late by searching longer. These models commit to a motion and hope it lands; they don't home in.

Why this happens

All five learn the same way — by copying human demonstrations. That method has a known weak spot, and this task sits directly on it.

The hardest moment is the rarest. In every demonstration the robot spends seconds reaching and carrying, and a split second doing the precise release. A model that averages demonstrations learns the long easy parts well and the brief exacting part badly — exactly the pattern measured above.
Averaging destroys precision. If demonstrations release the block at slightly different spots, the average release point can be somewhere no demonstration ever succeeded. Fine for carrying; useless for a matchbox.
Small errors compound. The robot acts on the consequences of its own last action. Slight drift while carrying lands it in a situation no demonstration covered, with nothing teaching recovery — so it circles the target instead of correcting.

What the compute actually bought

These models differ 80-fold in size. If scale were the answer, the scores would climb from left to right. Model size runs along the bottom on a ratio scale — each step is 10× more parameters, not 10 more.

Placement score against parameter count · vertical axis starts at 18
The line doesn't climb — it sags. Going from 52 million parameters to 4.1 billion, an 80-fold increase in size, buys you less score. Four of the five models sit on a flat, slightly downward band. Only GR00T breaks upward, and the model directly beside it in size, WALL-OSS, sits at the bottom — so the thing lifting GR00T plainly isn't scale.

Score per billion parameters

Turning that around: how much capability did each billion parameters actually deliver? The answers span nearly two orders of magnitude, so the scale below is ×10 per step — each gridline is ten times the last.

Read the gridlines, not the bar lengths. On a ×10 scale a bar twice as long is a hundred times the value, not twice it. ACT's bar is drawn about 1.6× SmolVLA's, but the real ratio is 9×; it is drawn about 2.5× GR00T's, and the real ratio is 41×. That compression is what lets all five sit on one chart and stay legible — the printed numbers are the authority, not the lengths.
An 88-fold gap in value for money. ACT returns roughly 493 points of score per billion parameters; π0.5 returns 5.6. Even GR00T, the winner, is 40× less efficient per parameter than the little control — it buys its lead by being 58× larger. If you are paying for inference on real hardware, that ratio is the number that matters.
What this comparison ignores. Parameter count is a crude stand-in for cost, and per-parameter efficiency flatters small models by construction. It also ignores the thing pre-training is for: none of this measures generalising to a new object, instruction or scene, which is where the large models are supposed to earn their size. On this one narrow task, they didn't.

Where this is weak

One task, one robot. A single pick-and-place job on one humanoid. Not a verdict on these models generally.

GR00T had home advantage. Pre-trained on this very class of robot. Its win means "best for humanoids", not "best overall".

Only one gap here is statistically solid. GR00T's lead over the control clears its margin; nothing else does. The ordering among the other four is not a ranking — treat those as tied.

Still only nine successes. Out of 750. The pass/fail column remains far too sparse to rank on, which is exactly why the main score measures distance rather than completion.

Modest adaptation. 216 demonstrations, 20,000 training steps each. With far more data the ordering could change.

Simulation only. No physical robot was involved, so nothing here proves real-world behaviour.
Bottom line. Open-source robot models already handle seeing and grabbing perfectly — 750 out of 750 attempts. What defeats them is letting go in the right place, and they miss sideways, not vertically. Four of the five park beside the target and run out the clock; GR00T instead gets closest of anyone and then drives straight past, which makes its failure a stopping problem rather than a seeing one. The largest model with the best training score finished last and never once completed the task; a 52-million-parameter model with no pre-training outscored all three of the other pre-trained systems; and the only model to beat that baseline convincingly was the one pre-trained on robots shaped like this one. Match the model to your robot's body, distrust training scores, and expect precise placement — not grasping — to be the wall.
