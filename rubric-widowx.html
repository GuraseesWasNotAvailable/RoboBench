Grading rubric · Zero-shot WidowX

How the zero-shot WidowX benchmark is graded

This is a separate benchmark from the G1 pick-and-place suite, with a different embodiment and a fundamentally different question. Here, models get no task-specific fine-tuning at all — they are run exactly as shipped. It measures out-of-the-box capability, not adapted capability.

1 · The question: zero-shot, not adapted

The G1 benchmark gives every model an equal adaptation budget — same demos, same steps — and asks "who adapts best to this body?". This benchmark asks the opposite: "what can you do with no adaptation at all?" Each policy is loaded from its public checkpoint and run directly on the tasks. No demonstrations, no fine-tuning, no per-model tuning.

Why both exist. A model can be strong out-of-the-box and poor at adapting, or vice-versa. Reporting them as one number hides that. These two benchmarks are complementary axes — shipped capability vs. adapted capability — and a policy earns a place on each independently.

Variants are paired across models — every policy sees the same 100 starting configurations per task, so a difference is the policy, not the draw.

4 · Scoring

Headline: official binary success

The primary metric is SimplerEnv's official binary success per episode — the task's own completion criterion, not a hand-rolled one. Per-model rate = successes ÷ 700; per-cell rate = successes ÷ 100.

Uncertainty: Wilson 95%

Every rate carries a Wilson score interval (correct near 0% and 100%, where the normal approximation breaks). Overlapping intervals on the same task mean a tie, and are reported as a tie — the 1.9-point gap between the top two models is exactly such a case.

Beyond binary: stage and trajectory diagnostics

Binary success undercounts partial progress, so each episode is also decomposed into stages — grasp → lift → transport → align → release → finish — plus trajectory diagnostics (path length, end-effector jerk, horizon use, recovery fraction, gripper-closed fraction). These show where a policy loses capability, not just that it did: most failures reach transport and then stall out at the step limit.

5 · Reading the results honestly

  • The mean hides specialists. Overall rates are close (Octo 34.9%, SpatialVLA 33.0%) but the task profiles are not — they win different tasks. A single "Bridge score" that averages an easy drawer with an unsolved sink is close to meaningless; the per-task fingerprint is the real result.
  • Sink does not decide rankings. Eggplant-to-sink is 0/400 — every model, every attempt. A task no one can do under this protocol carries no discriminative signal and is excluded from ranking claims.
  • Bigger is not better here. The 7B and 4B models do not dominate; a 93M generalist leads the average. Zero-shot capability is not a function of scale on this suite.

6 · What this benchmark does not measure

Adaptation — that's the G1 suite's job; nothing here fine-tunes. Real hardware — SimplerEnv is a real-to-sim approximation of Bridge scenes, so a sim-to-real gap remains and no real-robot claim is made. Anything outside these seven tasks — it is a snapshot of shipped checkpoints on one arm, not a general capability verdict.
